Welcome to THE ART

Where Art, History and Culture Come Alive

In a world rapidly shifting toward the digital, many stories risk fading into silence especially those rooted in smaller nations or cultures that have faced war, colonization, and loss. Cambodian art is one of those stories. Rich in spirit, but often under represented. Vibrant, yet overlooked.

THE ART was created to change that. This is a space to amplify Cambodian voices past and present. A space where artists, curators, students, and art lovers can connect through shared appreciation of Khmer creativity. Whether it’s a hand-woven silk, a temple relief, or a digital reimagining of Apsara mythology, every piece here contributes to the evolving story of Cambodia.

History of the National Museum

George Groslier (1887–1945), historian, curator and author was the motivating force behind much of the revival of interest in traditional Cambodian arts and crafts, and it was he who designed this building that is today ‘traditional Khmer’ architecture. The foundation stone for the new museum was laid on 15 August 1917. Some two-and-a-half years later, the completed museum was inaugurated during Khmer New Year on 13 April 1920.

Control of the National Museum and Arts Administration was ceded by the French to the Cambodians on 9 August 1951. In 1966 Chea Thay Seng was the first Cambodian director of the museum. During the Khmer Rouge regime (from 1975 to 1979), all aspects of Cambodian life including the cultural realm were devastated. The museum was abandoned and found in disrepair, its roof rotten and home to a vast colony of bats, the garden overgrown, and the collection in disarray. It was quickly tidied up and reopened to the public on 13 April 1979.
